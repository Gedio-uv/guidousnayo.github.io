Tolk — a German learning tool I built because the existing ones didn't fit how I learn.

Status

Context

Living in Leipzig and studying German, I kept bouncing off the existing vocabulary apps — they were either too gamified or too rigid. None gave me what I actually needed: fast word lookup with visual context, grammar I could reference on the go, and a way to practice recognition without committing to a full lesson.

So I built one. Tolk (formerly DeutschLernen) is a mobile-first web app for German learners, designed around real daily use rather than engagement metrics.

The Problem

Learning German as an adult living in Germany means needing answers fast — on the street, in a shop, reading a sign. Existing tools take too many taps to surface a simple answer, and none adapt to whether you're just starting out or already comfortable in the language.

One choice reshapes the whole app

The first screen asks for your level, and that single decision changes the interface language across the entire product — no setting buried in a menu.

  • Beginner (A1–B1)

    The interface speaks your native language, so the tool never becomes a second thing to decode.

  • Advanced (B2–C2)

    The interface runs entirely in German, turning everyday use into passive immersion.

  • Not sure?

    A five-minute placement test points you to the right starting level. It's all changeable later in Settings.

Surface 01 · Search

Search that meets you in either language

The search bar takes a word in German or in your own language and detects which one automatically — there's no mode to toggle. One result screen then answers the whole question, so the primary use case needs zero navigation.

Surface 02 · Word result

One word, one screen

A lookup returns the article and gender, IPA pronunciation with audio, the translation, synonyms, and example sentences — alongside a contextual image pulled from Unsplash that anchors the word to a real scene. Beispiele and Grammatik sit one tap away on the same result.

Surface 03 · Flashcards

Recognition over recall

The card game drills vocabulary through visual recognition rather than translation, across ten everyday categories — Animals, Food, Colors, Nature, Home, Body, Transport, Emotions, Travel, and Work. You see the image, name the word, and flip to confirm. Low friction, high retention.

Surface 04 · Grammar

Grammar you can scan, not study

Grammar is built as a quick reference, not a course — four sections for cases, articles, verbs, and word order. The declension tables are colour-coded by case, and any form that changes from the nominative is highlighted in gold, so the underlying pattern is visible at a glance instead of memorised cold.

Music — learning through lyrics

Plenty of people listen to music while learning a language, but matching the sounds you hear to the actual words is hard. The Music tab is a YouTube player that shows the lyrics of your favourite songs in German, so you can see what you're hearing — and it works both ways, rendering lyrics in the device's native language too. It also surfaces German-language recommendations to learn from.

How I built it

UX first, AI second. My focus was entirely on the UX — the flows, hierarchy, and information architecture. I delegated UI execution to AI tooling within a system I defined, trusting the tool with the visual layer while owning the structure. That's exactly how I approach AI-assisted design in product work.

Built for speed. Groq powers instant lookups, definitions, and example generation; the Unsplash API surfaces the contextual imagery; Antigravity handled UI generation and front-end assembly. The first working version came together in a single focused build, and it's grown new modes since.
